# Voice Profile — Prem Pratap Singh

Purpose: keep blog drafts consistent with how I actually write. Derived from my
own first-author scientific prose, not from AI output. Source samples analysed:

- Singh, P.P. et al. *Systematic investigation of aflatoxigenic Aspergillus flavus
  inhibition*, Int. J. Food Microbiology 450 (2026) 111632 — abstract, full
  introduction, full discussion, conclusion.
- Singh, P.P. et al. *Insights into the antimicrobial efficacy of Coleus aromaticus
  essential oil*, Food and Chemical Toxicology 182 (2023) 114111 — abstract, full
  introduction, discussion, conclusion.
- Singh, P.P., Gupta, V., Prakash, B. *Recent advancement in functional properties and
  toxicity assessment of plant-derived bioactive peptides using bioinformatic
  approaches*, Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition 63(20) (2023) 4503-4521 —
  first-author review. Register closest to a blog, since it surveys a field rather than
  reporting one experiment.

Two registers are described in each section: **Academic (what my papers do)** is
the anchor, and **Blog target** is how that trait should carry into a blog post.
The academic register is denser and more formal than a blog should be; the goal is
to keep the structural DNA while lowering the reading level.

---

## Quick reference (the DNA that must survive translation to blog)

1. Funnel openings: broad fact with citation, then narrow to the specific gap.
2. Mechanism as a causal chain: observation → function → hedged inference.
3. Define every abbreviation at first use, in parentheses.
4. Explanation first, evidence second.
5. Calibrated hedging; never overclaim; flag what still needs validation.
6. Close with significance plus an honest limitation or next step.

---

## 1. Average sentence length

**Academic:** Mean about 22 words, ranging roughly 11 to 28. Sentences rarely drop
below 10 words. Long sentences carry a trailing participial or relative clause, e.g.
"Aspergillus flavus is among the most resilient fungi that infect both pre-harvest and
post-harvest agricultural commodities, leading to significant losses in quantity and
quality." (24 words).

**Blog target:** Bring the mean down to about 16 to 18 words. Deliberately vary the
rhythm, which my academic writing does not do: use an occasional short sentence of 8
to 10 words for emphasis, then return to a longer explanatory sentence. Avoid three
long sentences in a row.

**Validated (calibration edit, July 2026):** short declarative sentences are welcome
("The chemistry works." "The regulation has a first example."). The short sentence is
fine; keep the *verb* inside it measured, not blunt or dramatic. See section 9.

## 2. Preferred paragraph length

**Academic:** Introduction paragraphs run 4 to 6 sentences (about 90 to 140 words).
Discussion paragraphs run longer, 6 to 9 sentences (150 to 220 words), because each
holds a full observation-to-inference arc.

**Blog target:** 3 to 5 sentences per paragraph. One idea per paragraph. Break the
long discussion-style block into two.

## 3. How I introduce new ideas

**Academic:** Topic sentence stating a general truth, supported immediately by
citations, then a narrowing move to the unmet need. Example arc: "The persistent
challenge of A. flavus contamination has led to the widespread use of synthetic
chemicals." then the drawbacks, then "These concerns underscore the urgent need for
alternative, sustainable antifungal strategies." The study is then introduced with a
bridge line: "Given this background, the present study explores..."

**Blog target:** Keep the funnel, drop the citation stack. Open a section with the
general point, give one concrete anchor, then state why it matters for grapevine or
plant-disease work. The bridge line equivalent is "What I take from this is..." or
"For my own work, this matters because...".

## 4. Level of technical detail

**Academic:** Very high. Exact concentrations (0.4 µL/mL), gene identifiers
(AFLA_001011), binding energies (−5.72 kcal/mol), wavelengths (260 nm), enzyme names.
Detail is used as evidence, placed after the claim.

**Blog target:** Keep one or two concrete numbers per section as anchors, because
specificity is a signature of my writing. Drop the gene-ID density and the reagent-
level detail. A reader should learn the magnitude of an effect without needing the
accession number.

## 5. Common transition words

**Academic (what I actually use):** "Similarly," "however," "In recent years,"
"Given this background," "Additionally," "Further," "Furthermore," "Thus," "In brief,"
"Following that," "Alongside," "Eventually," "Collectively," "Overall,". In review
writing I also open sections with "Nowadays," "Currently," and "In this context,".

**Blog target:** Keep the logical connectives that carry argument: "however," "so,"
"still," "even so," "that said," "in practice." Moderate the heavy academic openers
("Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "Overall") which read as filler in a blog
and are stripped by the humanizer anyway. Prefer connecting ideas through content
("This same logic applies to...") over a bare adverb.

## 6. How often I define terminology

**Academic:** Almost always at first mention, using an inline parenthetical:
"aflatoxin B1 (AFB1)", "essential oils (EOs)", "simplex-centroid mixture design (SCMD)",
"mitochondrial membrane potential (MMP)", "DNA damage response (DDR)", "radius of
gyration (Rg)". Very consistent; abbreviations are then reused.

**Blog target:** Same habit, but define in plain language before the abbreviation, and
only abbreviate a term that recurs. For a general reader, add a half-sentence of what
the term does, not just its expansion. Example: "long non-coding RNA (lncRNA), a
regulatory molecule that does not code for protein."

## 7. How I explain mechanisms

**Academic:** A three-beat causal chain: state the observation, state the function of
the component, then a hedged inference about what it means. Real example: "Genes for
tetrahydrofolate synthase... were all significantly upregulated. These enzymes are
involved in the folate-mediated one-carbon cycle, which is essential for DNA
replication, repair, and methylation. Their increase suggests that the fungus is
attempting to restore genomic stability... under metabolic stress." Inference verbs
are always hedged: "suggests," "likely," "indicates," "supports the hypothesis."

**Blog target:** Keep the same three beats, in plainer words: what was seen, what that
part normally does, what it probably means. Keep the hedge on the inference. This is my
most recognisable move and should be preserved intact.

## 8. How I end articles

**Academic:** Recap the finding, state its significance, then note a limitation or
future direction, in that order. This closing shape is the single most consistent
feature across all three papers. Real closes: "Collectively, these findings highlight
ZTC's effective, multi-target mechanism... makes it a promising candidate for
sustainable antifungal strategies." and "Overall, this study contributes to our
understanding... However, further research directed... needs to be carried out, to
address the possible adverse interaction with food matrix." The review closes the same
way: "Although the bioinformatics analysis provides an overview..., further research
needs to be carried out using animal studies to validate the in silico observation."
The recurring formula is: summary of what was covered, then "Although X gives us Y,
further work is needed to validate Z."

**Blog target:** Keep the pattern of summary, then significance, then an honest open
question, but written in first person and forward-looking: what I take from this, why
it matters for grapevine or plant-disease work, and what I would want to see next. End
on the open question, not a slogan. Do not close with a promotional or grand line.

## 9. Words and phrases to avoid

- No em dashes or en dashes as sentence punctuation. Use commas, colons, or semicolons.
- No AI buzzwords: leverage, synergy, passionate, cutting-edge, innovative, dynamic,
  spearheaded, robust, actionable, next-generation, groundbreaking, pivotal, holistic,
  delve, landscape (as metaphor), tapestry, plethora, myriad, seamless, unlock.
- No filler openers: "It is worth noting," "It is important to note," "Interestingly,"
  "Notably," "In today's rapidly evolving...".
- No rhetorical rule-of-three (three items for cadence). Genuine enumerated lists of
  three or more real items are fine and normal in my writing; the ban is on the
  decorative triple.
- No -ing padding: "Leveraging my expertise in..." Just state the thing.
- No promotional tone. Describe what was found, not how important it sounds.
- My own tell: in review writing I sometimes reach for ornate or inflated words
  ("burgeoning proficiencies," "astonishing," "decorous development," "game-changer,"
  "remarkable"). These are a real tendency in my drafting, which is exactly why they
  should be filtered for a blog. Prefer the plain word: "recent progress," not
  "burgeoning proficiencies"; "useful advance," not "game-changer".
- Verb temperature (calibration edit, July 2026): I prefer measured, standard verbs
  over blunt or colloquial ones. In a real edit pass I changed "killing broadly" to
  "aiming at a broad range of targets" and "stretch protection" to "extend protection".
  The rule: keep the verb neutral and technical. This is separate from sentence length;
  short sentences are fine, but the verb inside them should not reach for drama.

## 10. Typical reading level

**Academic:** Graduate or specialist. Dense noun phrases, Latin binomials, low Flesch
reading ease (college and above).

**Blog target:** Upper-undergraduate. Readable by a scientifically literate general
reader who is not a virologist. Keep the precision, lower the jargon load. Do not
dumb down to a school reading level; the audience is professional.

## 11. Tone when discussing uncertainty

**Academic:** Carefully calibrated. Hedged inference throughout ("likely," "suggests,"
"probably triggered," "appears unsustainable," "may help," "could be"), and explicit
statements that a result still needs validation ("computational studies warrant
experimental analysis to validate the results"). I never state an inference as a fact.

**Blog target:** Preserve this exactly. It is a core part of the voice. Separate what
was measured from what it might mean. Say plainly when something is a reasoned guess
versus an established result. This matches how I want to be read.

## 12. Active or passive voice

**Academic:** Split by function. Passive for procedures ("The MIC was determined,"
"samples were incubated"). Active for interpretation ("the fungus is attempting to
restore," "ZTC disrupted ergosterol biosynthesis," "these findings highlight").

**Blog target:** Lean active throughout, since a blog has few procedures to describe.
Use active voice for every claim and interpretation. Reserve passive only when the
actor genuinely does not matter.

## 13. Whether I cite examples before or after explanations

**Academic:** Explanation first, evidence second. I state the principle or claim, then
supply the specific data or identifier as support, often parenthetically: "chromatin
remodelers, such as ISWI (AFLA_011130)". The reader gets the meaning before the
detail.

**Blog target:** Keep this order. Make the point, then give the example or number that
grounds it. Never open with a raw data point and ask the reader to infer the meaning.

## Punctuation and mechanics

**Comma after an introductory phrase (calibration edit, July 2026).** I consistently
place a comma after a fronted adverbial, prepositional, or transitional opener. In one
edit pass I restored this comma five times: "over seasons, the pathogen population
adapts," "in principle, the pathogen takes it up," "For grapevine systems, the stakes
are specific," "In late 2023, the United States EPA," and "So, the honest summary is."
This matches my papers ("During storage, it produces...", "Given this background,
..."). Do not use the lighter comma-free journalistic style; when a sentence opens with
a scene-setting phrase, close that phrase with a comma before the main clause.

No em dashes or en dashes as sentence punctuation (already covered in section 9). Use
commas, colons, and semicolons.

## Signature moves to keep (these make it sound like me)

- The "Given this background" or "What I take from this" bridge from context to my
  own angle.
- Observation → function → hedged inference for any mechanism.
- One concrete number or named entity per section as an anchor.
- Define-on-first-use in parentheses.
- Closing on an honest open question and a next step, not a slogan.
- Separating measurement from interpretation, always.

## One-line self-check before publishing

Could a reader tell what was actually measured, what it probably means, and what I
still do not know? If those three are cleanly separated and the jargon is defined,
it sounds like me.
